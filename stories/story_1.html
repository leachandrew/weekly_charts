SEC Proposed Climate Disclosure Rule

Andrew Leach


The long-awaited SEC proposed climate change risk disclosure rule was published this week. The proposed SEC rule (PDF) document is long (510 pages) but the important parts are pretty much what we were expecting. The SEC guidelines draw extensively from the recommendations of the Task Force on Climate Related Disclosures (TCFD), mirroring many aspects of requirements in other jurisdictions. The major parts of the proposed rule (see page 42) require public companies to report on

  • The approach to oversight and governance of climate-related risks by the Board and executive team;
  • How climate-related risks are identified, assessed, and managed and how these processes are integrated into other risk management processes;
  • The climate related risks likely to impact financial statements, strategy, business model, and outlook in the short-, medium-, or long-term;
  • The impact of identified physcial and transition risks on financial outlook and statements;
  • Climate-related targets, goals, and transition plan(s).
  • Scope 1 (direct) and 2 (emissions from electricity, steam, heat, or cooling)
  • Scope 3 GHG emissions and intensity (indirect upstream and downstream emissions), if material, or if the company has set a Scope 3 emissions target; and
  • The registrants climate-related targets or goals, and transition plan, if any.134

The elements most likely to attract attention are the mandatory disclosure of Scope 1 and 2 emissions, as well as the link between targets and required disclosure for Scope 3 emissions. The latter, I would expect, will be of particular interest to banks since financed emissions would generally fall into that category, as well as to oil and gas companies wherein Scope 3 emissions would include emissions associated with the transportation and eventual combustion of produced hydrocarbons.

Canada is undertaking a similar process, with draft NI 51-107 rules published last year. Given how extensively Canadian oil sands companies have relied on the talking point that most (80%+) of the wells-to-wheels emissions from their products are downstream, expect the conversation around disclosure of Scope 3 emissions to be highly contentious.
